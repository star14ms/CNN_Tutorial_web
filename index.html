From MLP to CNN

Multi-Layer Perceptron

A Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) is the simplest deep network: every neuron in one layer connects to every neuron in the next. Hover over a neuron to see its connections.

MLP Diagram (4 → 4 → 3)

Each connection has an independent learned weight.

Why Images Are Hard for MLPs

A fully connected layer treats each pixel as independent. For a 28×28 image (784 inputs) and 128 hidden neurons, that's already 100,352 weights — just for one layer. Scale to 256×256 color images and the first layer alone needs over 25 million weights.

Parameter Explosion

weights in layer 1 alone
Key problems: too many parameters → slow training, overfitting. No spatial awareness — the network has no concept that pixel (3,4) is next to pixel (3,5). Not translation invariant — the same feature at a different position looks completely different to the network.

How CNNs Solve This

Convolutional Neural Networks introduce three structural ideas that make image learning tractable:

1Local Connectivity

Each neuron in a conv layer only looks at a small local region of the input (the receptive field), typically 3×3 or 5×5. This dramatically reduces the number of connections and biases the network toward detecting local patterns like edges and corners.

2Weight Sharing

The same kernel (filter) is slid across the entire image. Every position shares the same learned weights. A 3×3 kernel has only 9 weights regardless of image size — compared to millions in a fully connected layer. This also makes the network translation equivariant: the same feature at a different position produces the same response, just shifted.

3Hierarchical Features

Early layers detect simple patterns (edges, colors). Middle layers combine those into textures and shapes. Deep layers represent high-level concepts (eyes, wheels, faces). This hierarchy of features mirrors how the biological visual cortex processes images.

Convolutional Layer

A convolution slides a small kernel over the input image. At each position it computes a dot product between the kernel weights and the local input patch, writing the result to the output feature map.

Stride & Padding

Stride is how many pixels the kernel moves between applications.

Padding adds a border of zero-value pixels around the input before sliding the kernel.

Beyond CNNs: Diffusion & Generative Models

Everything so far has been a discriminative model — mapping an image to a label, box, or mask. Generative models flip the problem: instead of recognizing an image, they create one. Diffusion models are the current state of the art for image, video, and audio generation.

How Diffusion Models Work

Diffusion models learn to generate data by reversing a gradual noising process. Training happens in two phases:

Forward process: a training image is progressively corrupted by adding a small amount of Gaussian noise at each of hundreds of steps, until it becomes indistinguishable from pure static.

Reverse process: a neural network — usually a U-Net — is trained to predict and remove the noise added at each step. Once trained, it can start from pure random noise and iteratively "denoise" it into a coherent, realistic image.

Where Diffusion Models Are Used

1Media Generation & Synthesis

Text-to-image: systems like Stable Diffusion and DALL·E turn a written prompt into a novel, realistic image by guiding the denoising process with text embeddings. Video generation: the same idea is extended across time, keeping frames temporally consistent to produce moving clips from a prompt.

2Image Editing & Manipulation

Inpainting: erase part of an image and let the model fill the gap based on surrounding context or a text prompt. Image-to-image: start from a sketch or photo and prompt the model to restyle it, change the background, or add detail.

3Audio & Music

Waveforms (or their spectrograms) are treated like image pixels — diffusion progressively refines noise into high-fidelity speech or music.

4Specialized Industries — incl. Healthcare

Super-resolution: medical scans (MRI/CT) and surveillance footage are sharpened by diffusion upscalers, revealing detail invisible in the low-resolution input. Robotics & science: diffusion models predict complex physical dynamics for robot planning, and synthesize plausible molecular or material structures for drug and materials discovery.
